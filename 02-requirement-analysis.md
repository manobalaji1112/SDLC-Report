# Requirement Analysis in SDLC

## Introduction

Requirement Analysis is the first and one of the most important phases of the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC). In this phase, the development team gathers, analyzes, documents, and validates the requirements of the client or end users.

The success of a software project largely depends on how accurately the requirements are collected and understood. Incorrect or incomplete requirements can lead to project delays, increased costs, and customer dissatisfaction.

Requirement Analysis acts as a bridge between the client and the development team by ensuring that everyone has a clear understanding of what needs to be built.

---

# What is Requirement Analysis?

Requirement Analysis is the process of identifying, gathering, understanding, and documenting the needs and expectations of stakeholders for a software system.

The goal is to clearly define:

* What the software should do
* How the software should perform
* What constraints must be considered
* What business problems need to be solved

The output of this phase is usually a Software Requirement Specification (SRS) document.

---

# Objectives of Requirement Analysis

The major objectives are:

* Understanding customer requirements
* Defining project scope
* Identifying system functionalities
* Avoiding misunderstandings
* Reducing development risks
* Providing a foundation for design and development

---

# Importance of Requirement Analysis

Requirement Analysis is important because:

## 1. Reduces Project Failure

Clearly defined requirements help avoid confusion during development.

## 2. Saves Time and Cost

Identifying requirements early prevents expensive changes later.

## 3. Improves Software Quality

The development team builds exactly what the customer needs.

## 4. Enhances Communication

Clients and developers share a common understanding of project goals.

## 5. Supports Project Planning

Accurate requirements help estimate budget, resources, and timelines.

---

# Activities in Requirement Analysis

Several activities are performed during this phase.

## Requirement Gathering

Information is collected from stakeholders.

Methods include:

* Interviews
* Surveys
* Questionnaires
* Workshops
* Meetings
* Observation

---

## Requirement Analysis

Collected requirements are examined and organized.

The team identifies:

* Functional requirements
* Non-functional requirements
* Business requirements

---

## Requirement Documentation

Requirements are documented clearly and formally.

This document becomes the reference for future phases.

---

## Requirement Validation

The requirements are reviewed with stakeholders to ensure accuracy and completeness.

Any ambiguity is clarified before moving forward.

---

# Types of Requirements

## Functional Requirements

Functional requirements describe what the system should do.

Examples:

* User registration
* Login functionality
* Product search
* Online payment
* Report generation

These requirements define system behavior.

---

## Non-Functional Requirements

Non-functional requirements describe how the system should perform.

Examples:

* Security
* Performance
* Reliability
* Scalability
* Availability

Example:

"The application should load within 3 seconds."

---

## Business Requirements

Business requirements describe organizational goals.

Examples:

* Increase online sales
* Improve customer engagement
* Reduce operational costs

---

# Stakeholders in Requirement Analysis

Several stakeholders participate in this phase.

## Client

Provides business needs and expectations.

## End Users

Describe actual system usage and expectations.

## Business Analysts

Gather and document requirements.

## Project Managers

Manage scope and resources.

## Development Team

Provides technical feasibility analysis.

## Testing Team

Ensures requirements are testable.

---

# Software Requirement Specification (SRS)

An SRS document contains all project requirements.

It serves as an agreement between the client and the development team.

Typical contents include:

* Project overview
* Functional requirements
* Non-functional requirements
* System constraints
* User requirements
* Assumptions
* Acceptance criteria

---

# Characteristics of Good Requirements

Good requirements should be:

## Clear

Easy to understand.

## Complete

Contain all necessary information.

## Consistent

No contradictions.

## Testable

Can be verified through testing.

## Feasible

Technically achievable.

## Traceable

Can be tracked throughout development.

# Real-World Example

Consider an Online Shopping Application.

## Client Requirements

The client wants:

* User registration
* Login functionality
* Product catalog
* Shopping cart
* Online payment
* Order tracking

---

## Functional Requirements

* Users can create accounts.
* Users can search products.
* Users can add products to cart.
* Users can make payments.

---

## Non-Functional Requirements

* Website should load within 3 seconds.
* Payment transactions should be secure.
* System should support 10,000 users simultaneously.
